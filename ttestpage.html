In the plot from the previous page, one can see that each decade has always had more male artworks compared to the non-male (female and non-binary) artworks. To check if this is by random chance alone or not, data from the 1960s and 2000s where male artworks were significantly higher in number, and data from a decade on both sides of those were considered. A t-test was performed for each one.

A t-test basically estimates the true difference between two group means using the ratio of the difference in group means over the pooled standard error. With a t-test, there is one independent variable (gender with two groups/levels in this case) and one dependent variable (number of acquisitions in this case).

Before a t-test, an F-test for variance is used. The null-hypothesis H0, a statement that there is no significant difference or effect, for the F-test is given by H0: Variances of number of male and non-male artworks are equal.

Except for the 1990s, F-test results for data from every other decade resulted in F > F-critical (one-tail) and p-values (one and two-tail) < 0.05 significance level. So H0 was rejected to proceed with an unequal varaince independent t-test.

For the 1990s, F < F-critical (one-tail) and p-values (one and two-tail) were > 0.05 significance level. So H0 could not be rejected and an equal variance independent t-test was performed instead.

Irrespective of the type, H0 for the t-test: There is no difference between the mean number of acquisitions from male and non-male artists.

All t-values deviate enough from 0 to have an effect on the mean difference. That combined with the fact that all p-values (of one and two tails) are < 0.05 implies that H0 could be rejected for each of those t-tests.

It would suggest that the significant increase in the mean number of artwork acquisitions from male artists for each of those decades is also not likely due to random chance alone.


So, the null hypothesis was rejected when considering two "abnormal" samples (1960s and 2000s) in which a huge jump in the mean number of acquisitions for male-produced artwork was observed. It was also rejected for "normal" decades before and after those decades.


Thus, the overall trend of male artworks being more in number than non-male artworks at the MoMA over the years is not just due to random chance!
